WHAT THIS PACKET IS
-------------------
This is a small, self-contained test. Its only job is to let you SEE
the Blackletter gate work with your own eyes, before we ever connect it
to VERITAS.

Nothing in here touches VERITAS. Nothing here changes any of your other
work. You can open it, watch it, and close it, and everything else you
own is exactly as it was. This is a safe sandbox.

The gate does one thing: it takes a word and decides whether that word
is properly sourced in your founding-era references.
  - If the word IS sourced, the gate lets it through.
  - If the word is NOT sourced, the gate STOPS -- on purpose -- and
    says "Void for Vagueness," which just means: "this word does not
    have a clear enough source on file, so I will not use it and I will
    not guess."

That refusal is the whole point. It is the thing that forces honest
answers instead of confident guesses.


WHAT IS INSIDE THIS PACKET
--------------------------
You do not need to open or understand most of these. They just need to
sit together in the same folder. Here is what each one is and why it is
here:

  1. START_HERE.txt
     This guide. The thing you are reading now.

  2. TRY_THE_GATE.py
     The little test program you will start. This is the one you touch.
     It shows the gate deciding words.

  3. veritas_libraries.py
     The gate itself -- the part that does the deciding. TRY_THE_GATE
     asks this file to do the work.

  4. VERITAS_definitions_library.json
     Your plain founding-era definitions (Johnson 1755 / Bailey 1721).
     This is the collection the gate treats as the deciding one.

  5. etymology_dictionary.py
     Your word-origins collection. This can support a decision but never
     makes one by itself.

  6. VERITAS_historical_context_library.json
     Your historical-use and intent notes. Also support only, never
     the decider.

All six must stay in the same folder for the test to work. That is why
they are packed together here -- so you never have to hunt for them.


WHAT YOU ARE ABOUT TO DO, AND WHY
---------------------------------
You are going to unpack this folder onto your computer and start the
little test program. When it starts, it will show you the gate letting
some sourced words through and stopping some unsourced words. Then it
lets you type your own words to try. That is all. When you have seen it
work, you are done.


THE STEPS
---------
Do these in order. Each step says what to do, and why.

STEP 1 -- Save the packet to your computer.
  WHAT: Click the packet file named BLACKLETTER_GATE_PACKET.zip that I
        gave you, so it saves onto your computer.
  WHY:  This brings the whole test onto your machine in one piece.
  WHERE IT LANDS: It goes into your Downloads area, the place your
        computer keeps things you save from the screen.

STEP 2 -- Open your file viewer and find it.
  WHAT: Open your file manager. That is the program that shows your
        folders -- its icon looks like a small filing cabinet or folder.
        Then click the word "Downloads" on the left-hand side.
  WHY:  This is where the packet landed, so this is where you go to get
        it.
  YOU SHOULD SEE: a file named BLACKLETTER_GATE_PACKET.zip

STEP 3 -- Unpack the packet.
  WHAT: Right-click the file BLACKLETTER_GATE_PACKET.zip one time. A
        small menu appears. Click "Extract Here."
  WHY:  The packet arrives squeezed into one tight file so nothing gets
        lost or separated. "Extract" simply un-squeezes it back into a
        normal folder you can open. A ".zip" file is just that kind of
        squeezed package.
  YOU SHOULD SEE: a new folder appear named BLACKLETTER_GATE_PACKET
        (no ".zip" on the end this time).

STEP 4 -- Open the new folder.
  WHAT: Double-click the new folder named BLACKLETTER_GATE_PACKET to
        open it.
  WHY:  Everything the test needs is inside this folder, and the next
        step has to happen from inside it.
  YOU SHOULD SEE: the six items listed above, sitting together.

STEP 5 -- Open a typing window inside that folder.
  WHAT: Right-click an empty space inside this folder (not on any file,
        just on the blank area). A small menu appears. Click
        "Open Terminal Here."
  WHY:  A "terminal" is just a small window where you can type an
        instruction straight to the computer, one line at a time. You
        need it opened INSIDE this folder so the computer already knows
        where the test files are.
  YOU SHOULD SEE: a small window open, usually with a dark background
        and a blinking cursor, waiting for you to type.

STEP 6 -- Tell the computer to start the test.
  WHAT: Type the following line exactly, then press the Enter key:

            python3 TRY_THE_GATE.py

  WHY:  Reading that line out loud in plain terms:
        - "python3" is the name of a program already on your computer
          that knows how to read this kind of test file.
        - "TRY_THE_GATE.py" is the name of the test file you want it to
          read.
        - Typing them together with one space between, then pressing
          Enter, tells the computer: "Use Python to read the
          TRY_THE_GATE file now." That is what starts the test.

STEP 7 -- Watch it, then try your own words.
  WHAT: The window fills with the gate deciding words. First it shows a
        few examples. Then it says "Now you try it." When it does, type
        any word and press Enter to see the gate decide it. When you are
        finished, type the word  done  and press Enter.
  WHY:  This is the whole purpose -- to see, with your own eyes, that
        the gate lets sourced words through and stops unsourced ones.


WHAT SUCCESS LOOKS LIKE
-----------------------
You will see lines like these:

    Word:  senate
       ->  LET THROUGH
       Reason: a founding-era definition is on file, so this word is sourced.

    Word:  indictment
       ->  STOPPED   (the gate says: Void for Vagueness)
       Reason: there is NO founding-era definition on file for this word.

If you see sourced words getting LET THROUGH and unsourced words getting
STOPPED, the gate works. That is the whole test. You are done.


IF SOMETHING LOOKS WRONG
------------------------
  - If, after Step 6, the window says something like "command not found,"
    that means the Python program is not on this computer yet. Stop there
    and tell me -- I will walk you through adding it, in plain steps.

  - If it says it cannot find a file, it usually means the six items got
    separated. Make sure all six are sitting in the same folder, then try
    Step 5 again from inside that folder.

  - Anything else that looks off: copy the words you see in the window,
    tell me, and I will sort it out.


A NOTE ON WHICH COMPUTER
------------------------
These steps are written for your Linux computer. If you are doing this on
the Windows machine instead, the only thing that changes is Step 5 and the
exact word in Step 6 (Windows uses "python" instead of "python3"). Tell me
you are on Windows and I will hand you that version -- everything else is
the same.
