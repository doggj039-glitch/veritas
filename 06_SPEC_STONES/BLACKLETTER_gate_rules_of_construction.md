# BLACKLETTER — THE GATE'S RULES OF CONSTRUCTION

*What the gate does when a proposal reaches it. These are the rules — the only thing the gate holds.*

*Version 1 — captured from decisions made in build sessions. Each rule is traced to its source. Nothing here is invented.*

*Read the Architecture Map first. This document lives inside "Part 3 — The Gate."*

---

## WHERE THESE RULES COME FROM

These rules are not house style. They are the rules of construction — the long-standing discipline for reading a legal instrument. They are sourced, not asserted.

- **Blackstone**, *Commentaries on the Laws of England*, Introduction, Section 2 ("Of the Nature of Laws in General"), 1765. The five signs for reading the maker's will: the words, the context, the subject matter, the effects and consequence, and the spirit and reason. Read as of **the time the law was made.**
- **Elphinstone**, *Rules for the Interpretation of Deeds*, 1889. The numbered code — most directly Rule 8 (expressed intention = the meaning of the words used), Rule 10 (primary meaning, as of the time of execution), Rule 11 (extrinsic evidence only to find meaning), Rule 14 (contemporaneous interpretation; do not declare void for uncertainty until examined with all contemporaneous light).

A rule that cannot be traced to a source has no authority here. That applies to these rules too.

## THE RULES

### Rule 1 — Read every text in the register of its own time.

- Read each text in the standard, common register of the date it was written.
- This is almost always the plain, ordinary meaning — because that is how people wrote. The founders wrote in standard meaning, not legal-technical meaning.
- A technical (term-of-art) sense applies **only** where that sense was already the standard usage at that date.
- **Legal-first reading is forbidden.** To open in a modern legal register is to import a later meaning onto an earlier text. That is not interpretation — it is the very drift the gate exists to catch. The gate cannot itself commit the error it detects.
- Source: Blackstone, sign 1 (usual and most known signification; general and popular use; terms of art excepted); Elphinstone, Rule 10 (primary meaning, sensible as of the time of execution).

### Rule 2 — Classify the sense before judging it.

- Before the gate measures anything, it must first settle **which sense** the text actually used, in its own register.
- Do not assume the sense that has the most drift attached just because it is the most interesting to measure. The gate is drawn toward the driftier reading; resist it.
- Resolve the sense that was actually used, then judge only that.
- Source: the classify-before-judge decision; guards against judging a meaning that was never in play.

### Rule 3 — No meaning is controlling until two independent points converge.

- **Etymology is the base layer — the evidence gate.** A root is a claim to be corroborated, never a meaning trusted on its own. (This is the gate's most trusted input, which is exactly why it must be the most strictly verified.)
- A meaning becomes controlling only when a **second independent point** agrees:
  - Johnson 1755, where Johnson has the word; and/or
  - documented usage at the time (how the word was actually used at the founding).
- **Two points now. Three points later** (etymology + Johnson + usage).
- **Convergence = controlling meaning. Divergence = a finding.** When the points disagree, the gate reports the disagreement. It does not resolve it by preference. Divergence is often where the drift story begins.
- Source: the two-point verification decision; the same "don't trust one source" discipline the whole system runs on.

### Rule 4 — Extrinsic evidence only to fix meaning. Nothing else votes.

- Outside sources (dictionaries, etymology, usage records) are admissible for one purpose: to determine the primary meaning of the words. For no other purpose.
- **Legal interpretation is demoted.** A legal interpretation is not a meaning — it is a *claim about* a meaning. It never controls. It enters only as part of the drift record, tagged as a claim, measured against the standard.
- Source: Elphinstone, Rule 11 (extrinsic evidence only to find meaning, "for no other purpose whatsoever").

### Rule 5 — Findings, never verdicts.

- The gate reports deviation as fact. It reports the distance between the founding meaning and a later reading.
- It never grades merits, motive, blame, or intent. Cold as a caliper.
- It shows the drift. It does not judge it. The reader judges.
- Source: the findings-not-verdicts decision.

### Rule 6 — When a correction is needed, lead with the valid sense.

- Do not open a correction with negation ("no," "actually," "but").
- Open with the sense in which the reading is valid — stated plainly and briefly — then extend to the next sense, in the order the word actually drifted.
- The correction rides in on the timeline, not on a contradiction. Present the senses in the order they happened; the order does the diplomacy honestly.
- This works because drift is ordered: the valid founding sense first, the drifted sense after.
- **The one exception:** where there genuinely is no valid sense to lead with, do not fake an affirmation to be gentle. State it plainly, sourced, no false cushion. The format must never lie to protect a feeling.
- Source: the correction-protocol decision; the delivery side of findings-not-verdicts.

### Rule 7 — Do not declare void until examined with all contemporaneous light.

- If a meaning cannot be sourced, the gate halts it: **void for vagueness.** The gate refuses rather than guess.
- But it does not declare void *early.* It first examines the word with everything the period evidence can furnish — etymology, Johnson, documented usage.
- Only after that light is exhausted does "void for vagueness" apply.
- Source: Elphinstone, Rule 14 note ("the law will not declare a deed void for uncertainty until it has been examined with all the light which contemporaneous facts may furnish").

### Rule 8 — Intention means the meaning of the words used — not secret purpose.

- The gate reads intention as *what the words meant*, not as a guess about what was privately intended.
- No mind-reading. Findings come from the text and its period meaning, never from imputed motive.
- Source: Elphinstone, Rule 8 (expressed intention = the meaning of the words employed).

## THE ORDER OF THE GATE'S ANSWER

The gate's output is austere by design. It commits to the anchor and holds the rest.

1. **The etymological root** — always first. The origin. The anchor.
2. **Johnson 1755**, if the word is in the sourced set. The founding-era standard meaning. If it is not there, the gate says so plainly — it does not reach for a substitute.
3. **Then it stops.**

Everything later — Webster, Bouvier, OED-by-date, Black's — is **drift, available on request.** The gate does not volunteer the whole timeline. It hands over the anchor and the founding meaning, and holds the rest behind the door: *there is more if you want to see how far it has moved.*

- **Default view:** root, then Johnson, then a held door. Short.
- **Expanded (drift) view:** the full dated timeline — offered only when the person chooses to look. When shown, it flows down from the root, each entry stamped with its source and date.
- **Empty is shown as empty.** A cell with no sourced meaning must openly read *unsourced / not yet verified.* A blank must never look like a filled one. The format shows its holes.

Why the restraint: the moment several definitions sit side by side, the eye treats them as equal candidates for "the meaning," and the drift flattens into a menu. Leading with root + Johnson keeps the founding meaning from ever sitting at eye level with a later one. Asking for the drift makes seeing it an act of intent — so it reads as distance, not as an alternative meaning.

---

## THE ONE THING TO REMEMBER

The gate holds rules, and only rules. It reads a text in its own time, settles the sense before judging it, admits no meaning without two converging sources, keeps legal interpretation out of the meaning and in the drift record, reports distance without grading it, corrects by leading with the valid sense, refuses rather than guesses — and hands back the root, then Johnson, then a held door.
