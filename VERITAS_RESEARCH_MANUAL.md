# VERITAS — How to Research Anything
### A First-Timer's Field Guide to the Data Gatherers

*You are holding a research instrument, not a verdict machine. VERITAS does not tell you
"this is constitutional" or "this is illegal." It does something more useful and more
honest: it gathers the documented record — what the words meant when they were written,
how far the meaning has moved since, how the change happened, and what it has cost —
and hands it to you already sourced, so you can see the pattern for yourself and ask the
real question. The books in your library (like **A Long Train of Abuses**) are what these
gatherers produce. This guide teaches a complete beginner to run them.*

---

## PART 1 — What you are actually doing

Most legal talk asks: *"Is this allowed?"* — and answers with an opinion.

VERITAS asks a different, checkable question:

> **Read by the exact rules the founders used to write the words, what did this word / law /
> ruling / right actually mean — and how far, measurably and with sources, has the practice
> drifted away from that meaning?**

That question has four documented parts, and every finished piece of research contains all four. Keep these four boxes in your head — the whole method is just filling them with sourced facts:

| Box | The plain question it answers | Where the data comes from |
|---|---|---|
| **1. BASELINE** | What did the words mean at the founding? | VERITAS's **offline record** (free, no internet) — Johnson's Dictionary 1755/1773, founding-era usage, the founding voices |
| **2. DIVERGENCE** | What does it mean / how is it applied *now* — with numbers? | VERITAS's **reasoning tier** (web-sourced) + your own source list |
| **3. CASCADE** | *How* did it change — what was the first step, and what built on it? | The case record + the reasoning tier |
| **4. COST** | Who was harmed, and by how much — documented? | Public data (GAO, FDA, EPA, Census, court records) via the reasoning tier |

The finished product is not a verdict. It is a **documented, open question** — stated in plain
English, with every number traceable to public record, and with your own reasoning clearly
labeled as *reasoning*, not fact. That honesty is the whole point. (Look at how *Why Is This
Allowed?* ends: *"not to offer a legal conclusion… the sources behind these examples are
public record."* That is the tone you are aiming for.)

## PART 2 — Meet the data gatherers

VERITAS gathers in two modes. A beginner should learn the **free offline gatherers first**,
because they never cost money, never need internet, and can never make something up — and
only then turn on the **reasoning gatherer** for the modern half.

### The offline gatherers (free · no internet · cannot fabricate)

These only ever hand you **verbatim** text from a fixed, citable source. If they have
nothing, they say so — they never fill a gap with a guess.

- **The Dictionary gate** — the founding-era meaning of any word, *verbatim* from Johnson's
  Dictionary (the controlling baseline), plus every later dictionary's version in date order
  (this is the **"Define the Drift"** button), plus the Supreme Court cases that later
  construed the word.
- **The Concordance** — every place a word actually appears across the founding-era corpus
  (Federalist, the debates, Blackstone, Story…), quoted with its citation. This is how you
  see how the founders *used* a word, not just how a dictionary defined it.
- **The Founding Voices** — ten grounded scholars you can put a question to:
  *Federalist (Publius), Anti-Federalist, Blackstone, Story, Montesquieu, Rawle, Sedgwick,
  Lieber, the Convention, the Ratification debates.* Each answers **only** with verbatim,
  cited passages from its own sources. Ask two opposing voices the same question and the
  disagreement between their sourced words *is* the evidence of what the founders contested.

> **Why "verbatim only" matters:** it is the anti-fabrication rule. A dictionary quote you
> can look up is evidence. A paraphrase is just someone's opinion wearing evidence's clothes.
> VERITAS refuses to paraphrase founding-era meaning — so anything it hands you from the
> offline record, you can cite.

### The reasoning gatherer (needs an API key · costs cents · web-sourced)

This is the newer tier. It lets the instrument **reason and assemble** a full answer — but
under a strict contract that keeps it honest. When you ask it a question it returns **three
clearly labeled sections**, and it is *built so it cannot cross the streams*:

- **FOUNDING** — the founding-era half. Every point here is pulled **only** from the offline
  record above (the gate / concordance / voices). It is physically unable to state a
  founding-era meaning from its own memory.
- **MODERN** — the present-day half. Every point here comes **only** from a live web search
  and is **cited and marked as modern** (i.e. as drift away from the baseline).
- **ARGUMENT** — the reasoning that connects the two, **labeled as inference** — your
  analysis, clearly flagged as *not* a sourced fact.

> **Cost, in plain terms:** the offline gatherers are always free. The reasoning gatherer is
> off until you add a key, and then a single question costs a few cents (there's a **depth
> dial** — *quick* is nearly free, *deep* does more web searching for a bit more). The screen
> always shows you the cost. You are never surprised.

## PART 3 — The seven rules the founders used (your reading lens)

Before you gather anything, learn the lens. These are the actual rules 18th-century lawyers
used to read a document. You do not need a law degree — each is one plain idea. VERITAS
gives you the evidence to apply each one.

1. **List it or lose it** *(expressio unius)* — if a document names specific things, anything
   *not* named was left out **on purpose**. → *Ask VERITAS:* what does the text actually list?
   What's missing? Treat the absence as deliberate.

2. **Capital letters = defined words** — in founding-era drafting a Capitalized word is a
   *defined term*, not decoration. → *Do this:* look up every capitalized word in the
   Dictionary gate and use **that** meaning, not today's.

3. **Company you keep, category you're in** *(ejusdem generis)* — a general word that follows
   a list of specifics is limited to the *same kind* as the specifics. → *Example:* "person"
   sitting next to "citizen, life, liberty, property" is limited to the human category those
   words establish.

4. **Known by its neighbors** *(noscitur a sociis)* — read a disputed word by the words right
   around it. → *Do this:* run the word through the **Concordance** and read the surrounding
   text; let the neighbors confirm or narrow the meaning.

5. **No wasted words** *(surplusage)* — every word carries meaning; if two different words
   appear, they mean two different things. → *Example:* the 14th Amendment says "persons"
   *and* "citizens" separately — so they are not the same thing.

6. **Words mean what they meant then** *(original public meaning)* — a term's meaning is fixed
   at the date it was written; later re-definitions are *drift*, not the meaning. → *This is
   literally what "Define the Drift" shows you* — the meaning at the founding, then each later
   shift, in order.

7. **Only Article V may change the structure** — the Constitution changes one way only: a
   two-thirds vote of Congress and three-fourths of the states. A structural change made any
   *other* way (a court, an agency, a single statute) bypassed the only valid mechanism —
   flag it. → *Ask VERITAS's cases* whether a doctrine entered by amendment or some other route.

## PART 4 — The workflow, start to finish (with a worked example)

We'll research one thing all the way through so you see each gatherer in action. Our subject:
**does the word "person" in the 14th Amendment include a corporation?** (This is the spine of
*Why Is This Allowed?* — follow along and you'll rebuild that chapter yourself.)

### STEP 1 — Pick your subject and write the plain question
Anything can be the subject: a single **word** ("commerce," "person," "religion"), a **clause**,
a **ruling**, a **doctrine** (qualified immunity, the third-party doctrine), or a **pattern**
(who gets prosecuted for the same rule). Write your question in one plain sentence.

> **Our question:** *"When the 14th Amendment says 'person,' does that include a business
> corporation — read by the rules used when it was written?"*

### STEP 2 — Gather the BASELINE (offline · free)
Get what the words meant at the founding. Use the offline gatherers:

- **Dictionary gate → look up "person."** You get Johnson's verbatim definition ("a human
  being"). Click **Define the Drift** to see how later dictionaries moved.
- **Concordance → "person."** Read how the founders actually used it, in context (Rule 4).
- **Founding Voices → ask two.** Put your question to the **Federalist** *and* the
  **Anti-Federalist** (or **Story**, who wrote the Commentaries). You get verbatim, cited
  passages — and where they disagree, that's the live seam.

> **What you now have (all citable):** the verbatim 1755/1773 meaning of "person," the
> surrounding-word context, and founding voices on it. That is Box 1, sourced.

### STEP 3 — Read it by the seven rules
Now apply the lens from Part 3 to what you gathered:
- **Rule 2:** "Person" is a defined term → use Johnson's "human being."
- **Rule 3 & 4:** it sits beside "citizen, life, liberty, property" → the human category.
- **Rule 5:** "persons" and "citizens" appear separately → distinct human statuses, neither a
  business.
- **Rule 1:** corporations are **not named** in the amendment or its ratification debates →
  excluded on purpose.

Write down, in plain English, what each rule shows. This is your founding-era finding.

### STEP 4 — Gather the DIVERGENCE and COST (reasoning tier · web-sourced)
Now the modern half — with numbers, sourced. Turn on the reasoning gatherer and ask it your
question at **normal** or **deep** depth. It returns:
- **FOUNDING** — confirms your baseline *from the record* (Johnson, the cases).
- **MODERN** — the current doctrine, **web-cited**: *Santa Clara* headnote (1886), *Bellotti*
  (1978), *Citizens United* (2010), Hobby Lobby (2014)… each with a source and marked modern.
- **ARGUMENT** — the labeled reasoning about the gap.

Then gather the **cost data** the same way (as *The Measurable Divergence* and *The Price of
Harm* do): ask for the documented numbers — corporate rights rulings over time, qualified-
immunity plaintiff success rate, representation ratio (30,000:1 → 770,000:1), fine-to-profit
ratio — **each with a public source you can open.**

> **The iron discipline here — copy it from your own files:** every number gets a source line.
> *The Measurable Divergence* ends with a "How to verify every number" section listing
> Cornell, OpenSecrets, the Census Bureau, the Brennan Center, Violation Tracker, the GAO.
> **Do that.** If you can't point a reader to where a number lives in the public record, don't
> use the number.

### STEP 5 — Trace the CASCADE (how it happened)
A drift always has a *first step* and a *chain that built on it.* Ask the reasoning gatherer to
trace it: the 1886 reporter's **headnote** → courts citing the headnote as if it were the
ruling → a century of decisions building on that → today's settled doctrine. Then apply
**Rule 7:** did this enter through Article V? No — so flag it as a change made without the only
valid mechanism. **Be precise and fair:** note that it *is* settled doctrine today (a real
legal fact) *and* that it never went through the amendment process (also a fact). Both are true;
say both.

### STEP 6 — Write it up honestly (the three labels + the open question)
Assemble your chapter in the shape your books already use:
- **FOUNDING** — what the words meant, each point tagged to its record source.
- **MODERN** — what it means / how it's applied now, each point tagged to its web source and
  marked as modern.
- **ARGUMENT** — your reasoning about the drift, **clearly labeled as your inference.**
- **THE OPEN QUESTION** — end like *Why Is This Allowed?* does: not a verdict, but a
  documented, fair question the people with authority should answer in public. State plainly
  what you're *not* claiming (you're not saying anyone planned it; you're not deciding the law).

That's a finished piece — baseline, divergence, cascade, cost, open question — every number
verifiable, your reasoning honestly flagged.

## PART 5 — The five rules that keep it trustworthy

Break these and it stops being research. Keep them and a stranger can check your work.

1. **Verbatim or nothing** for founding-era meaning. Quote the Dictionary/voice; never
   paraphrase what a word "basically meant." (VERITAS enforces this — trust it.)
2. **Every number gets a source line.** No exceptions. If you can't cite where it lives in the
   public record, cut it.
3. **Label the three registers.** Founding (record) vs. Modern (web) vs. Argument (your
   inference) must never blur together. The reasoning tier does this for you — preserve it.
4. **Absence is deliberate.** If the text doesn't list something, that silence is data (Rule 1)
   — but say "the record is silent," don't invent what it "would have" said.
5. **Separate the legal fact from the open question.** "It is settled doctrine" and "it should
   be revisited" are *different sentences.* State the doctrine accurately, *then* ask the
   question. Never dress an opinion up as a holding.

## PART 6 — Your first practice run (do this start to finish)

Pick an easier word than "person" and walk all six steps once:

1. **Subject:** the word **"commerce"** in Article I ("regulate commerce among the several
   states"). **Question:** did "commerce" originally mean *all economic activity*, or something
   narrower?
2. **Baseline (offline):** Dictionary gate → "commerce" (Johnson: *"intercourse; exchange of
   one thing for another"*). Click **Define the Drift.** Concordance → "commerce." Ask the
   **Federalist** about the commerce power.
3. **Read by the rules:** what category do the neighboring words put it in?
4. **Divergence (reasoning tier, `normal`):** how did the meaning expand — *Gibbons* (1824),
   *Wickard* (1942), *Lopez* (1995)? Each **web-cited, marked modern.**
5. **Cascade:** what was the step that widened it, and what built on it?
6. **Write-up:** FOUNDING / MODERN / ARGUMENT / open question — every number sourced.

When you've done that once, you have the whole method. Everything else is the same six steps
on a bigger subject.
